Electrosurgery (Diathermy) Laboratory: Olympus UES‑40 (Monopolar Modes)

In this lab you will safely operate a monopolar electrosurgical unit (ESU) (Olympus UES‑40), perform controlled output power and crest factor measurements with a calibrated Electrosurgery Analyzer (Fluke QA‑ES III), capture representative waveforms on an oscilloscope, and relate waveform modality (Pure Cut, Blend, Uro, Coag 1/2, Spray) to tissue effect (cutting vs hemostasis) using a surrogate tissue sample (pork chop: fat, vascular muscle, bone). Only monopolar functions are used in this exercise.

Learning Objectives

  • Identify functional controls, connectors, indicators, and safety accessories of the UES‑40 ESU.
  • Explain how waveform duty cycle, peak voltage, and crest factor influence tissue effect (cut efficiency vs coagulation).
  • Measure output power accuracy at specified set points using a 400 Ω test load.
  • Determine crest factor for each selected mode and interpret differences.
  • Capture and describe oscilloscope waveforms for Pure Cut, Blend, Uro, Coag 1, Coag 2, and Spray modes.
  • Adjust mode / settings to optimize cut vs hemostasis on differing tissue types (fat, vascular muscle, near bone) while observing safe activation duty cycle (<10 s per minute).

Safety & Warnings

  • The active electrode can cause thermal injury (burn). Never touch it directly or indirectly (via metal instruments) while the unit is powered.
  • Limit activation to <10 seconds per minute to avoid overheating / overloading the generator.
  • Do not change analyzer impedance or measurement range while RF output is active (prevents analyzer damage).
  • Ensure the return (dispersive) electrode simulation / connection on the analyzer is correctly engaged before activation.
  • Keep flammable materials (alcohol prep, drapes) away from the active field; allow drying time after any cleaning.
  • Use smoke evacuation (RapidVac) when producing sustained vapor to reduce aerosol exposure.
  • Verify proper grounding / isolation per local electrical safety requirements (full leakage testing is outside this specific lab scope).

Equipment

  • Electrosurgery Analyzer (Fluke QA‑ES III) with 400 Ω test load (and other selectable loads if available).
  • Olympus UES‑40 electrosurgical unit (monopolar accessories: active handpiece, simulated dispersive electrode via analyzer).
  • Oscilloscope (Keysight InfiniiVision) with appropriate high‑frequency probe or RF attenuator (observe bandwidth & voltage limits).
  • Smoke evacuator: RapidVac (tubing & filter set).
  • Pork chop (distinct fat, muscle, bone regions) as tissue surrogate.
  • Data sheet (table for set power, measured power, crest factor, mode, notes).
  • PPE: lab coat, gloves, eye protection (smoke / splatter mitigation).

Background Summary

Electrosurgery applies high‑frequency alternating current (hundreds of kHz) to tissue causing resistive (Joule) heating. Cutting requires rapid heating to vaporization producing incision with minimal lateral thermal spread (continuous waveform, relatively low peak voltage, lower crest factor). Coagulation favors intermittent bursts with higher peak voltage, allowing protein denaturation and hemostasis (higher crest factor, lower duty cycle). Crest factor = (Vpeak / Vrms) is an indicator of spikiness; coag modes typically elevate crest factor to spark and desiccate tissue.

Different named modes modify duty cycle (burst / pause pattern), envelope modulation, and sometimes target voltage control. The analyzer internally converts delivered RF into equivalent power & crest factor values using resistive loads; real patient impedance is dynamic and not purely resistive.

Pre‑Measurement Checklist

  1. Inspect ESU handpiece, cable insulation, and analyzer leads for integrity.
  2. Connect ESU active output to analyzer input; connect dispersive return simulation per analyzer manual.
  3. Select 400 Ω test load (as specified for power verification) and confirm analyzer displays correct load.
  4. Power on ESU and analyzer; allow warm‑up (per manuals) if required.
  5. Ensure smoke evacuator ready (filter installed, suction tubing near tissue surrogate position).
  6. Prepare oscilloscope: set coupling (likely 10:1 probe, appropriate vertical scale), time base to capture several RF bursts (e.g., a few ms/div) or envelope, plus secondary slower scale for duty cycle observation.
  7. Prepare data table with columns: Mode, Set Power (W), Load (Ω), Measured Power (W), Crest Factor, Peak Voltage (if captured), Comments (cut quality / spark / char).

Power & Crest Factor Measurement Procedure

  1. Mode Familiarization: Cycle through Pure, Uro, Blend, Coag 1, Coag 2, Spray at low power (e.g., 30 W cut, 20–30 W coag) observing analyzer readouts briefly (<2 s each) to see qualitative differences.
  2. Cut Modes Quantitative Test: For CUT settings: 150 W Pure, 150 W Uro, 125 W Blend (adjust if device uses discrete steps). For each:
    • Set analyzer to 400 Ω.
    • Arm smoke evacuator (optional for short bursts).
    • Activate ESU for ~2–3 s (stay <10 s/min). Record measured power and crest factor (stabilized mid‑activation).
    • Repeat once for reproducibility; calculate % deviation: (Measured − Set)/Set × 100.
  3. Coag Modes Quantitative Test: For COAG settings: 60 W Coag 1, 60 W Coag 2, 60 W Spray.
    • Repeat measurement steps above (short bursts; Spray may show rapid arcing and higher crest factor).
  4. Oscilloscope Capture: Using analyzer’s waveform output (monitor / scope port), capture representative waveforms for each mode. For cut, capture continuous sinusoidal (or modulated) segments; for coag modes, capture burst trains. Document frequency (approx.), burst period, duty cycle, and peak amplitude.

Tissue Interaction (Pork Chop Exercise)

  1. Place tissue on insulated, non‑conductive surface; position smoke evacuator nozzle <2–3 cm from site.
  2. Using Pure cut at a moderate setting (e.g., 50–70 W first), make a brief incision through muscle; note incision quality (smoothness, lateral char).
  3. Repeat incision with Blend and Uro: compare edge coagulation and char depth.
  4. Apply Coag 1 then Coag 2 to a small bleeding surrogate area (light pressure not required here; observe surface whitening / shrinkage differences).
  5. Use Spray mode briefly over fat vs muscle; observe surface carbonization and penetration difference.
  6. Near bone area (cartilaginous / harder region): evaluate effect—avoid prolonged activation to prevent excessive thermal spread.
  7. Record qualitative observations for each mode & tissue type (columns: Mode, Tissue Region, Effect Quality, Lateral Thermal Spread, Smoke Density, Notes).

Measurements & Analysis

  1. Power Accuracy: Compute % deviation for each measurement; confirm within manufacturer specification (e.g., ±(20% + 5 W)—verify actual spec in documentation).
  2. Repeatability: For one mode (Pure) repeat three times; calculate mean & standard deviation of measured power.
  3. Crest Factor Comparison: Rank modes by crest factor; correlate with observed tissue char / hemostasis.
  4. Duty Cycle Estimation: From scope waveform, estimate on-time vs period for Blend and Coag modes; relate to thermal effect.
  5. Energy Density Discussion: Qualitatively discuss why continuous waveforms produce cleaner cuts while interrupted bursts produce coagulation.

Documentation (Running Protocol)

  • Date/time, ESU model & serial, analyzer model & calibration date.
  • Ambient conditions (approx. temperature, note if mains supply stable).
  • Mode & settings table (as above) with measured power & crest factor.
  • Waveform screenshots/photos labeled with mode.
  • Tissue interaction observations table.
  • Any anomalies (e.g., unexpected power fluctuation, analyzer overload warning) plus corrective actions.

Reflection & Questions

  1. Which mode exhibited the highest crest factor, and how did tissue effect differ?
  2. How would an incorrect dispersive (return) electrode placement manifest clinically and in analyzer readings?
  3. Why must analyzer load not be changed during activation?
  4. What modifications might be needed when operating near implants or metallic staples?
  5. Suggest additional quality control tests (e.g., leakage currents, alarm function) to achieve a full periodic maintenance protocol.

Common Pitfalls

  • Overlong activation causing thermal overload or inaccurate power reading due to duty cycle limits.
  • Changing analyzer settings mid-activation (risk of damage or erroneous crest factor).
  • Incorrect load impedance selection invalidating power comparison.
  • Oscilloscope probe attenuation mismatch leading to clipped waveforms.
  • Insufficient smoke evacuation increasing exposure to surgical plume.

Checklist (Sign Off)

  • Controls & connectors identified (monopolar only used).
  • Power & crest factor recorded for specified modes.
  • Waveforms captured & documented.
  • Tissue surrogate tests performed (fat, muscle, near bone) with notes.
  • All activation periods within duty cycle safety (<10 s/min).
  • Reflection questions answered; device powered down; area cleaned; ESU left safe.
