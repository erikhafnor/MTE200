Infusion Pumps Laboratory

Learning Objectives

  • Differentiate design and performance characteristics of a volumetric (peristaltic/linear) infusion pump versus a syringe infusion pump.
  • Configure and execute standardized flow accuracy and occlusion pressure tests using a Fluke IDA-5 Infusion Device Analyzer.
  • Interpret rate, deviation (%), time, and peak pressure outputs and relate them to manufacturer specifications and IEC 60601-2-24 concepts.
  • Evaluate air-in-line (bubble) detection performance and upstream (negative) pressure alarm functionality.
  • Explain clinical implications of flow accuracy, line compliance, occlusion alarm settings, and air detection sensitivity for patient safety (infiltration, under-/over-infusion, embolism risk).
  • Document results in a clear, reproducible laboratory protocol suitable for quality assurance.

Safety & Warnings

  • Use only de‑ionized water (simulation fluid). No medications in this exercise.
  • Never bypass air-in-line alarms on clinical devices in patient care; here you intentionally introduce air only because the set is disconnected from any patient.
  • Ensure all tubing is fully primed (no bulk air) before starting quantitative flow tests—entrained air distorts volumetric accuracy and occlusion timing.
  • Wipe spills immediately; keep electrical connectors dry.
  • Follow the Fluke IDA‑5 user manual warnings; do not exceed specified pressure / vacuum limits when provoking alarms.

Equipment

Background

Volumetric infusion pumps typically employ linear peristaltic or cassette mechanisms delivering discrete stroke volumes; syringe pumps advance a plunger using a leadscrew/stepper system. Key performance parameters include:

  • Flow Accuracy (% deviation): Difference between programmed and measured mean rate over a defined test volume/time.
  • Occlusion Pressure & Alarm Delay: Time for downstream pressure to rise to alarm threshold after a distal blockage; influenced by compliance (syringe elastomer, tubing stretch) and pump mechanism.
  • Air-in-Line Detection: Ultrasonic, optical, or pressure-based detection of bubbles; smaller detection thresholds reduce embolic risk but can increase nuisance alarms.
  • Upstream (Negative) Pressure Monitoring: Detects inlet restrictions or empty fluid source (collapse / partial vacuum) to prevent under-infusion.

Interpreting results requires understanding that syringe pumps often show shorter occlusion alarm delays (lower stored elastic energy) and potentially higher short-term flow variability at very low rates, while volumetric pumps may average pulses over time.

Pre‑Lab Preparation

  1. Read user & service manual sections for Infusomat Space and Perfusor Space describing: flow accuracy specification, occlusion pressure setting levels (0–9), air sensor, upstream sensor (negative pressure), and calibration references.
  2. Review Fluke IDA‑5 manual (priming procedure, template creation: Utilities > Edit Templates).
  3. Prepare a results sheet or spreadsheet with the tables provided below.

System Setup

  1. Program Infusomat (volumetric) with total volume 250 mL, rate 100 mL/h (Channel 1).
  2. Program Perfusor (syringe) with syringe volume 50 mL, rate 100 mL/h (Channel 2).
  3. Prime each administration set completely (remove air) using pump prime functions and auxiliary priming syringes (fill priming syringes with ~15 mL de‑ionized water beforehand).
  4. On the IDA‑5 perform necessary priming to clear internal lines (manual procedure starting at page ~7 in the user manual).

Create Analyzer Test Templates (on IDA‑5)

Create two templates (one can be reused, but label clearly) with the following steps (mirroring the Norwegian specification):

Step 1  Type: Flow   Rate: 100 mL/h   Vol/Press: 25 mL   Time: 02:30   Tol%: 5
Step 2  Type: Occlusion  Rate: 200 mL/h  Vol/Press: 487 mmHg  Time: 01:00  Tol%: 5
  1. Utilities > Edit Templates > Add > Enter parameters for Step 1 and Step 2 as above; save.
  2. From channel overview: Setup > Template > select template > Start > enter arbitrary control number.

Procedure

  1. Start Flow Test (Step 1): After template start the analyzer prompts for priming. Deliver ~5 mL until IDA‑5 shows Auto Start. Select Auto Start, then start the pump. Repeat for Channel 2 (ESC back to overview).
  2. Record analyzer displayed rate, deviation (%), elapsed time, peak pressure for each channel (use Table 1).
  3. Transition to Occlusion Test (Step 2): When volume reached ("volume reached"), press Next. Adjust the pump programmed rate to 200 mL/h. Start the pump infusion first, then start Step 2 on the analyzer.
  4. Allow pressure rise until occlusion alarm triggers; capture peak pressure and time to alarm. (Analyzer may stop automatically at press limit.)
  5. Document occlusion alarm level setting (e.g., Infusomat level code 0–9 and displayed mmHg, if shown) and compare to measured occlusion pressure.
  6. Air-in-Line Tests: Disconnect volumetric pump distal line from analyzer (after completing quantitative tests). Install a syringe pump set under the drop sensor (as per Norwegian instructions), distal end into a collection beaker. Introduce two bubble volumes sequentially:
    • Small: 0.02–0.30 mL
    • Larger: ~1.5 mL
    Use 300 mL/h rate. Observe and document alarm behavior, detection latency, displayed message (Table 3).
  7. Upstream (Negative) Pressure Alarm: Gently restrict or elevate source bag / pinch upstream line to create a partial vacuum until "upstream" / negative pressure alarm occurs (threshold approx. -120 mbar). Record method, time, and displayed pressure (Table 4). Do not apply excessive force (avoid damaging sensors).

Analysis Questions

  1. Compare flow accuracy between the volumetric and the syringe pump. Which was closer to the programmed rate? Provide quantitative difference and hypothesize causes (mechanism, compliance, calibration state).
  2. Explain mechanical and fluidic reasons the occlusion pressure profile and time to alarm differ between the two pump types.
  3. Are occlusion pressure limits universal for all patients? Discuss patient size, vascular access, infiltration risk, and why adjustable levels exist.
  4. On Infusomat Space: correlate selected occlusion level (0–9) to measured mmHg. Is mapping roughly linear? Does it match service manual values?
  5. From the Perfusor Space service manual: identify the tool/procedure used by biomedical engineers to verify or calibrate "cut-off" pressure. Summarize briefly.
  6. Evaluate air detection performance: Did the pump alarm for the smaller bubble? Discuss clinical trade-off between sensitivity and nuisance alarms.
  7. Assess the upstream negative pressure alarm activation: Was threshold near -120 mbar? Factors that could shift activation point?
  8. Identify primary uncertainties (measurement resolution, priming residual air, temperature) that could influence your accuracy results.

Reflection

  • How would prolonged unnoticed downstream occlusion impact delivered dose for a potent drug (e.g., vasoactive agent)?
  • What design improvements could reduce time-to-occlusion alarm latency?
  • Discuss how integrating predictive pressure trending could improve safety versus threshold-only alarms.

Common Pitfalls

  • Insufficient priming causing spurious flow deviation or delayed pressure rise.
  • Not reprogramming rate before Step 2 occlusion test (using 100 instead of 200 mL/h).
  • Failing to note occlusion level setting before test (cannot interpret pressure afterward).
  • Introducing air while still connected to analyzer during quantitative flow step (invalid measurement).
  • Applying excessive force for upstream test, risking sensor damage.
